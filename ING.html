To value residential fixed-rate mortgages, an accurate prediction of future prepayment rates is crucial. The attached data sets (mortgage_data.xlsx and refinancing_rate_data.xlsx) contain prepayment data.

The Excel file mortgage_data.xlsx contains the following variables: Client_ID: Unique identifier. Each client gets his own ID.

Date: the date at which we observe the prepayment rate and the other rates.

PP_rate (%): the prepayment rate in percentages.

Starting_notional: the contracts initial notional (the amount the client borrowed initially).

Client_rate (%): the rate in percentages the client must pay.

Loan_start_date: the start date of the mortgage contract.

Loan_end_date: the maturity date of the mortgage contract.

Remaining_maturity (M): the remaining maturity of the contract in months.

Outstanding_notional: the outstanding notional at the date.

The values are end of the month.

The time window ranges from January 2016 to December 2018.

The Excel file refinancing_rate_data.xlsx contains the following variables:

Date: the date at which we observe the prepayment rate and the other rates.

Refinancing rate (%): the rate in percentages for which the bank can sell a new comparable mortgage with similar characteristics to the ones in the file mortgage_data.xlsx.

Part I. Data analysis

We begin by analyzing the data and identifying potential issues. Notably, observations with zero outstanding notional could distort our findings. Therefore, we remove these entries to ensure data integrity. Next, we merge two Excel files and calculate the difference between the client rate and the refinancing rate. This step is grounded in financial theory: for fixed mortgage rates, a lower refinancing rate incentivizes clients to refinance their existing mortgages.

Simulate the interest rate path by means of the Vasicek model:

In[27]:
def vasicek_simulation(r0, kappa, theta, sigma, T, steps):
    """
    The Vasicek Model to simulate the interest rate path
    """
    dt = T / steps
    rates = [r0]
    for _ in range(steps):
        dr = kappa * (theta - rates[-1]) * dt + sigma * np.sqrt(dt) * np.random.normal()
        rates.append(rates[-1] + dr)
    return rates[1:]

theta = rr_.mean()     #long-term average
r0 = rr_.iloc[-1]
kappa=0.1              # mean reversion

date_range = pd.date_range(start='2019-01-01', end='2025-12-31', freq='M') 

rate_paths = np.array([vasicek_simulation(r0, kappa, theta, sigma=0.01, T = len(date_range)/12, steps=len(date_range)) for _ in range(100)]) # Simulate 10 years of refinancing rates
rate_path = np.mean(rate_paths, axis=0)
rr = pd.DataFrame(rate_path, index=date_range, columns = ['Refinancing rate'])

Add the columnns with the refinancing rate forecast:

Given our rate prediction, we forecast the PP rate. We assume that from now on the implied (contractual) payment is calculated by the formula of a Fixed-Rate Loan Payment:

$$ P = \frac{r \cdot PV}{1 - (1 + r)^{-n}} $$

Sum of all the discounted future payments (those under terms plus prepayment) is the theoretical value of the mortgage
